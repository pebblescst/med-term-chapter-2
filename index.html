Chapter 2 establishes the spatial language of medicine — how to precisely describe WHERE something is in or on the body. All directional terms assume anatomic position: patient standing erect, face forward, arms at sides, palms facing forward, toes forward. Every directional term depends on this universal reference point.

Organism
cyt/o
Cells
Smallest functional unit of life. Components: cell membrane, cytoplasm, lysosome, ribosome, mitochondrion, nucleus. Metabolism: anabolism (building up — ana-) + catabolism (breaking down — cata-) = metabolism (meta-).
hist/o
Tissues (4 types)
Epithelial (lining/covering) · Connective (support) · Muscular (movement) · Nervous (signaling). Parenchymal = does the organ's work. Stromal = supportive framework. Histology = study of tissues.
viscer/o
Organs
Also called viscera (singular: viscus). Parts: apex (apic/o — pointed tip), body (corpor/o — largest part), fundus (fund/o — base), fornix (fornic/o — arched part). One organ may function in multiple systems.
system/o
11 Body systems
Musculoskeletal · Integumentary · Gastrointestinal · Urinary · Reproductive · Blood/Lymphatic/Immune · Cardiovascular · Respiratory · Nervous/Behavioral · Special Senses · Endocrine. Maintained by homeostasis (home/o + -stasis).
Anatomic position — the universal reference
Standard anatomic position
Patient standing erect, face forward, arms at sides, palms facing forward (supinated), toes pointed forward. This is the frame of reference for ALL positional and directional terms in medicine. Without this shared reference, "anterior" and "posterior" lose their meaning.
Quick-reference: directional term pairs
Anterior ↔ Posterior
Anterior = front (anter/o). Posterior = back (poster/o). Synonyms: ventral (front) and dorsal (back). Example: sternum is anterior to the spine.
Superior ↔ Inferior
Superior = above (super/o). Inferior = below (infer/o). Direction synonyms: cephalad (toward head) and caudad (toward tail). Example: heart is superior to stomach.
Medial ↔ Lateral
Medial = toward midline (medi/o). Lateral = away from midline (later/o). Example: big toe is medial to little toe.
Proximal ↔ Distal
Proximal = closer to origin (proxim/o). Distal = farther from origin (dist/o). Example: elbow is proximal to wrist; wrist is distal to elbow.
Superficial ↔ Deep
Superficial = toward body surface. Deep = toward interior. Example: skin is superficial; bone is deep to skin.
Unilateral ↔ Bilateral
Unilateral (uni- = one side). Bilateral (bi- = both sides). Ipsilateral (ipsi- = same side). Contralateral (contra- = opposite side).
All directional terms assume anatomic position. Terms are presented as opposite pairs — the most effective way to learn them. Many pairs have synonyms used interchangeably in clinical practice.

Dorsal

Above and below
Superior ↔ Inferior
Superior
super/o
Above. Abbreviated sup. Synonym direction: cephalad. Example: heart is superior to stomach.
Inferior
infer/o
Below. Abbreviated inf. Synonym direction: caudad. Example: stomach is inferior to diaphragm.
Cephalad ↔ Caudad
Cephalad
cephal/o + -ad
Toward the head. Direction of superior/upward. -ad = toward. Used in imaging and embryology.
Caudad
caud/o + -ad
Toward the tail. Direction of inferior/downward. More precise on animals where "up" is ambiguous.
Midline and side
Medial ↔ Lateral
Medial
medi/o
Toward midline. Example: big toe is medial to little toe; nose is medial to eyes.
Lateral
later/o
Away from midline, toward side. Example: ears are lateral to nose; little toe is lateral to big toe.
Same side and opposite side
Ipsilateral ↔ Contralateral
Ipsilateral
ipsi-
Same side. ipsi- = same. Right arm and right leg are ipsilateral. Most cranial nerve functions are ipsilateral.
Contralateral
contra-
Opposite side. Strokes cause contralateral deficits — right brain damage = left side weakness.
Unilateral ↔ Bilateral
Unilateral
uni-
One side only. Unilateral hearing loss (one ear). Unicycle = one wheel.
Bilateral
bi-
Both sides. Bilateral pneumonia (both lungs). Bicycle = two wheels. ⚠ bi- (two) ≠ bi/o (life).
Near and far from origin
Proximal ↔ Distal
Proximal
proxim/o
Closer to origin/attachment. Elbow is proximal to wrist. For organs: closer to beginning.
Distal
dist/o
Farther from origin/attachment. Wrist is distal to elbow; hand is distal to wrist.
Surface and interior
Superficial ↔ Deep
Superficial
toward surface
Toward body surface. Skin is superficial to muscle. A superficial wound involves only the skin layer.
Deep
toward interior
Away from surface, toward interior. Bones are deep to muscles. A deep wound reaches muscle or bone.
Body position
Supine
lying face up
Lying on back, face upward. Used for many exams and surgical procedures. Palm face up = supinated.
Prone
lying face down
Lying on abdomen, face down. Used for posterior procedures, spine surgery. Palm face down = pronated.

Body cavities, the 9 abdominopelvic regions, 4 quadrants, and 5 body planes — the spatial reference systems used in clinical documentation and imaging.
Body cavities
Dorsal (posterior)
Cranial + Spinal
Cranial cavity (crani/o): within the skull; contains brain. Spinal cavity (spin/o): within vertebral column; contains spinal cord. Both are continuous and protected by bone.
Ventral (anterior) = TAP
Thoracic + Abdominal + Pelvic
Thoracic (thorac/o): within rib cage; heart + lungs + mediastinum. Abdominal (abdomin/o): below diaphragm; lined by peritoneum; digestive organs. Pelvic (pelv/i): within pelvic bones; bladder + reproductive organs + rectum. TAP mnemonic!
Thoracic subdivisions
Mediastinum + Pleural + Pericardial
Mediastinum (mediastin/o): central partition; contains heart, trachea, esophagus, great vessels. Pleural cavities (pleur/o): right and left; each contains a lung. Pericardial cavity: within pericardium (around the heart).
Abdominal features
Diaphragm + Peritoneum
Diaphragm (diaphragm/o, phren/o): muscular dome separating thoracic from abdominal cavity; primary breathing muscle. Peritoneum (peritone/o): membrane lining the abdominal cavity. Parietal = lines walls; visceral = covers organs.

5 body planes
PlaneDescription & clinical use
Sagittal planeVertical plane dividing body into LEFT and RIGHT portions (not necessarily equal). Multiple sagittal planes exist at various positions.
Midsagittal planeSpecific sagittal plane dividing the body into EQUAL left and right halves — exactly through the midline. Passes through nose, navel, and spine. Also called the median plane.
Frontal plane (Coronal)Vertical plane dividing body into ANTERIOR (front) and POSTERIOR (back) portions. Runs side to side. Coronal MRI brain images are in this plane — show left/right and up/down.
Transverse plane (Cross-sectional)Horizontal plane dividing body into SUPERIOR (upper) and INFERIOR (lower) portions. Also called axial plane. CT scans typically produce transverse plane images. At any level of the body.
Oblique planeAny plane cutting diagonally — not along a standard axis. Some imaging and surgical approaches use oblique planes for optimal visualization.

Chapter 2 has only 7 prefixes and 2 suffixes — the fewest of any chapter. These prefixes create many of the most important directional terms in clinical medicine.
Prefixes (7)
PrefixMeaningExamples & notes
bi-two, bothBilateral (both sides), biceps (two heads), bifurcation (splitting into two). ⚠ bi- (two) ≠ bi/o (life/living — as in biopsy, biology). Context rules!
contra-against, oppositeContralateral (opposite side), contraindication, contraception. Example: brain strokes cause contralateral deficits.
epi-above, upon, onEpigastric (above stomach — name of abdominopelvic region), epidermis (upon the skin), epicardium (upon the heart). Introduced Ch.1; used here for the abdominopelvic region.
ipsi-sameIpsilateral (same side). Latin prefix ipsi- = same. Most cranial nerve functions are ipsilateral.
meta-change, beyond, afterMetabolism (energy transformation), metastasis (cancer spread beyond primary site), metamorphosis (form change).
mid-middleMidsagittal (middle sagittal = equal left/right division), midline (center line of body), midclavicular.
uni-oneUnilateral (one side only). Think: unicycle = one wheel vs bicycle (bi- = two wheels).
Suffixes (2)
SuffixMeaningExamples & notes
-adtoward, in the direction ofCephalad (toward head), caudad (toward tail), dextrad (toward right), sinistrad (toward left). -ad indicates DIRECTION OF MOVEMENT. ⚠ -ad (direction) ≠ -al (pertaining to).
-umstructure, tissue, thingMediastinum (structure in the middle of chest), pericardium (around heart), peritoneum, cranium. Latin: plural = -a (cranium → crania; peritoneum → peritonea).
Look-alike pairs to distinguish
bi- vs bi/o
bi- = TWO (bilateral, biceps, bifurcation). bi/o = LIFE (biopsy, biology, antibiotic). Completely different word parts — the hyphen vs slash notation distinguishes them in study materials.
-ad vs -al
-ad = TOWARD (direction of movement): cephalad, caudad. -al = PERTAINING TO (descriptive): cranial, lateral. "Moving cephalad" = moving toward the head. "Cranial nerve" = nerve pertaining to the cranium/skull.
meta- in context
meta- = change/beyond/after. Metabolism = energy CHANGE. Metastasis = cancer goes BEYOND the primary site. Metaphysis = region BEYOND the growth plate. The specific meaning varies by term.
pelv/i vs pelv/o
pelv/i is the standard combining form for the pelvis — it uses 'i' instead of the usual 'o' as the combining vowel. This is an uncommon exception. Watch for it: pelvic (pelv/i + -c) not "pelvoc."

Anatomical region terms
TermDefinition & clinical notes
AbdominalPertaining to the abdomen (abdomin/o + -al). Abdomen = region between diaphragm and pelvis. ⚠ ≠ gastric (which refers to the stomach organ).
CranialPertaining to the cranium/skull (crani/o + -al). Cranial cavity (dorsal) contains the brain. 12 cranial nerves originate in the brain.
EpigastricAbove the stomach (epi- + gastr/o + -ic). Upper middle abdominopelvic region. Common site of pain in GERD and pancreatitis.
Frontal planeVertical plane dividing body into anterior and posterior portions. Also: coronal plane. Coronal MRI brain images are in this plane.
HypochondriacBelow the cartilage (hypo- + chondr/o + -iac). Right and left upper lateral abdominopelvic regions. Everyday meaning derives from ancient belief this region caused anxiety about illness.
HypogastricBelow the stomach (hypo- + gastr/o + -ic). Lower middle abdominopelvic region. Contains bladder, uterus, sigmoid colon.
InguinalPertaining to the groin. Right and left lower lateral abdominopelvic regions. Clinical: inguinal hernia (most common hernia type).
LumbarPertaining to the lower back. Right and left middle lateral abdominopelvic regions. Lumbar vertebrae = L1-L5. Lumbar puncture = spinal tap.
Midsagittal planeVertical plane dividing body into EQUAL left and right halves through the exact midline. Passes through nose, navel, and spine. Also: median plane.
PelvicPertaining to pelvis (pelv/i + -c). Pelvic cavity = inferior ventral cavity containing bladder, uterus/prostate, rectum. ⚠ pelv/i uses 'i' not 'o'.
SpinalPertaining to the spine (spin/o + -al). Spinal cavity = dorsal cavity within vertebral column containing spinal cord.
ThoracicPertaining to chest/thorax (thorac/o + -ic). Thoracic cavity contains heart + lungs. Enclosed by ribs, sternum, and thoracic vertebrae (T1-T12).
Transverse planeHorizontal plane dividing body into superior and inferior portions. Also: axial or cross-sectional plane. CT scans produce transverse plane images.
UmbilicalPertaining to the navel (umbilic/o + -al). Central abdominopelvic region. Umbilical hernia = herniation through umbilical ring.
VisceralPertaining to the organs (viscer/o + -al). Visceral peritoneum covers organs; parietal peritoneum lines cavity walls. Visceral pain is poorly localized and often referred.
